Training Notes

Week 1 Recovery
The week after a half marathon your legs need time regardless of how you feel. No quality, no doubles, long run capped at 6 miles. If the half was tough, stay at the low end all week. The marathon build is 14 weeks — there is no value in rushing back.
Doubless
Used on Monday and Wednesday only, never Saturday or the day before it. Monday is always capped at 5 miles total (3 AM + 5 PM = 8 mi max). Wednesday AM is 3 miles easy; PM builds to 7 miles by Week 6. Separate AM and PM by at least 6 hours. Skip the AM run if you're run-down — volume is a tool, not a rule.
Marathon Pace (Thu)
MP begins Week 5 with just 2 miles at goal pace, building to 5 miles by the peak weeks. Start conservatively — MP should feel controlled and rhythmic, not labored. These aren't tempo runs. Sustained, honest marathon pace teaches metabolic efficiency at race effort better than anything else in the plan.
Long Run Progression
Build-week long run peaks step up by no more than 2 miles: 10→12→13→14→15→16→18→20. Early weeks are set higher than a typical plan to leverage the half marathon base. Week 11's 18-miler is a deliberate bridge to 20 — the Wednesday 7-mile PM run creates pre-fatigue so both peak long runs simulate late-race fatigue. Run all long runs easy.
Strength Phases
Weeks 1–6: 3×5 linear progression. Week 7 onward: maintenance — same weight, no Olympic lifts. Week 13: mini sessions, no deadlifts. Week 15: final symbolic lift, very light. No deadlifts in the final 3 weeks — highest systemic fatigue cost of any lift and you need that energy for running.
Deload Weeks (4, 7, 10, 13)
No doubles. Long run drops significantly. Thursday is easy running on weeks 4, 7, and 10. Week 13 is a deliberate pre-taper flush — after the 18 and 20-milers, your body needs a real step-back before the final taper. The MP run stays in at a reduced dose to keep legs calibrated.
Taper & Race (Weeks 14–16)
Week 14 includes a short MP run to keep the legs sharp. Week 15 is race week — stay loose, eat well, sleep. Saturday is a 2-mile shakeout with strides; the race is Sunday. Arriving with 20-mile long runs behind you is a meaningful confidence advantage. Most coaches recommend at least 3–4 weeks of easy-only running before resuming any structure after the marathon.
