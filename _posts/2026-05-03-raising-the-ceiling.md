## Quality with a purpose

Quality work targets the upper end of your aerobic capacity, at your lactate threshold (LT2) and above it. Different sessions work different parts of that range, and the adaptations differ too. Intervals, short maximal efforts above LT2, are the most direct and time-efficient stimulus for VO2max gains, the ceiling on how much oxygen your muscles can use at full effort — though easy aerobic volume builds the same ceiling over time, particularly for newer runners. Raise that ceiling and you can work harder. Tempo work targets a different adaptation: raising your lactate threshold, which determines how long you can sustain a hard effort before things fall apart. Both make you a faster runner. They just aren't the same stimulus.

The two are designed to work together. Without the aerobic base that easy running develops, quality work has nothing to build on. Without quality work, the base never translates into speed. Seiler's research found that elite endurance athletes concentrate their hard work into roughly two sessions per week and keep everything else genuinely easy, 80 percent easy, 20 percent hard. The context is different for a recreational runner, but the prescription lands. Most training plans understand this in theory. Most runners undermine it in practice.

## The prerequisite

Quality requires a consistent aerobic base. If easy runs still feel hard, quality has no foundation to build upon. A reasonable signal that you're ready is running consistently 4–5 days a week, 30 to 45 minutes a day, and finishing those runs feeling like you could have gone further. This gives you the volume you need to start and the recovery you need to reap the benefits.

You can only go genuinely hard if easy days have left you recovered enough to get there. This sounds obvious until you realize how many quality sessions are quietly wasted because the runner arrived carrying fatigue from easy days that crept above the aerobic threshold (LT1). I've made that mistake all too often, and I've paid the price in forced time off. The problem was never the workout; it was the compromised easy days beforehand. Deliberately training under fatigue has its place in structured plans, but that's a different thing from arriving at a quality session carrying fatigue you didn't intend to accumulate. If easy days crept above aerobic threshold, the quality session is already compromised before it starts. I'd rather skip it than spend the effort in the black hole. Show up ready, and quality pays off.

## Genuinely hard

Quality sessions target an RPE 7 to 9. Labored but controlled. The feel differs by session. At tempo effort, you could answer a direct question in a few words, but you wouldn't want to sustain a conversation. At interval effort, speaking is genuinely impossible. If you're doing a tempo run and can't get a word out, you're working too hard. If you're doing intervals and could hold a sentence, you're not working hard enough. The upper end, RPE 9, is reserved for short, maximal efforts. Most quality work lives in the 7 to 8 range: hard enough to produce a strong adaptation signal, controlled enough to execute well.

The common failure mode isn't going too hard. It's arriving fatigued, drifting into the black hole, and backing off the intensity to something manageable. You're spending the session at RPE 5 or 6, harder than easy, not hard enough to matter. If you're running intervals and could hold a conversation between reps, you're probably not working hard enough.

## Types of quality work

Intervals are short, hard repeats at RPE 8 to 9, an effort you couldn't sustain much beyond a few minutes, with enough recovery between reps to hit the next one at the same intensity. They develop VO2max and raw speed. They're the most demanding to execute well, and they should stay infrequent.

Tempo runs are sustained efforts at or near your lactate threshold (LT2), typically 20 to 40 minutes at RPE 7 to 8. They train your body to sustain effort at the upper edge of what's controllable, just below the point where things fall apart. Most recreational runners undershoot this without realizing it, arriving at an effort that feels comfortably hard but sits closer to LT1 than LT2. Tempo effort should feel controlled early and earn its difficulty by the end. If the final few minutes didn't feel like work, you likely ran it closer to LT1 than LT2.

Hill repeats are short, hard efforts up a moderate grade, typically 30 seconds to two minutes, with an easy jog back down as recovery. They build strength and power with reduced ground impact than flat intervals at the same effort.

## The prescription

Start with one quality session per week. A tempo run is the right entry point for most runners: sustained, controlled, hard enough to matter without the complexity of managing reps and recovery. Every quality session should start with at least ten minutes of easy running to prepare yourself for the hard effort. Then you get comfortable with being uncomfortable.

Programming quality depends on your experience and total weekly volume. If you're running four days a week, one quality session is probably enough. A more experienced runner with higher volume might rotate types based on where they are in their training cycle and their goal race. The details matter, and they vary enough by runner that they deserve their own treatment.

The engine and the ceiling are two thirds of the picture. The long run is where easy effort and accumulated time on feet do something neither quality work nor short easy runs can do on their own. That's where we're headed.
