Garmin says my lactate threshold heart rate is 175 bpm and my threshold pace is 9:22 per mile.

One of those numbers is wrong. The other is accidentally right. Neither one came from a method I'd trust with my training.

<!--more-->

## The race

The OBX Lighthouse 5K is a summer series on the Outer Banks. Hot, flat, honest. No place to hide.

I ran it in June 2025 and came back for it in June 2026. Sub-30 was the goal, and the results tell a story.

2025: 30:15. An aggressive start and a heart rate spike that never recovered.

2026: 29:00. A controlled effort through the first two miles and a final kick that my training earned.

## The phantom threshold

That 2026 race was a genuine all-out effort. Thirty minutes of discomfort, a finishing kick with nothing held back, the whole thing.

My peak heart rate was 169 bpm.

Garmin thinks my lactate threshold is 175.

That's a problem. A threshold heart rate you can't reach in a hard 5K isn't a threshold. It's a ceiling invented by an algorithm and smoothed by a model that's never watched you suffer.

If your training zones are built on a phantom LTHR, every boundary shifts upward. Your easy runs run hot. Your threshold work gets miscalibrated. Your polarized model, if you're running one, gets quietly undermined one slightly-too-hard recovery run at a time.

## The pace

Garmin's threshold pace estimate for me is 9:22 per mile.

The field-derived number, calculated from the final 20 minutes of that same race, is also 9:22 per mile.

Same answer. Completely different methods. Only one of them is defensible.

Garmin got there by averaging my overall race pace, a blend of the cautious early miles, the grinding middle, and the finishing kick. The Joe Friel 20-minute method got there by isolating the sustained effort portion of the race: the segment where I was working at or near threshold long enough to measure it. 

If you're using Garmin's pace figure to set your tempo targets, you're trusting a method that happened to land correctly once. That's not the same as a method that's right.

## Estimating lactate threshold

The Friel field test protocol is straightforward: run a hard 30-minute effort, and take your average heart rate from the final 20 minutes. That's your LTHR estimate. The logic is that by the 10-minute mark of a hard sustained effort, you've burned off the anaerobic contribution and cardiac lag. What's left is a reasonable picture of where you can sustain output aerobically before lactate starts accumulating faster than you can clear it.

You don't need a lab. You need a race, or a hard time trial, and a GPS watch that can show you HR over time.

From that 2026 race, my LTHR comes out to 164 bpm. Against Garmin's 175, that's not a rounding error. That's a different zone structure entirely.

## Estimating aerobic threshold

Lactate threshold (LT2), the point where you cross into unsustainable effort, is what the Friel method measures directly. Aerobic threshold (LT1), where lactate first begins to rise above baseline, is harder to isolate without a lab at a cost that exceeds a good pair of shoes.

The field estimate finds LT1 between 80-90% of LTHR. For me, the math offers an estimate between 131 and 148.

At 148 bpm I'm comfortable. The shift in RPE, the rate of perceived exertion, doesn't show up until somewhere around 152 bpm. Perceived ventilatory shift is real physiological data. When your breathing pattern changes, lactate clearance is changing with it. My subjective experience puts LT1 within four beats of my calculated LT1 celing.

The cleanest way to validate aerobic threshold is a talk test: run at progressively increasing pace and note exactly where fully comfortable conversation becomes controlled conversation. For me, that line is around 152. Your formula may vary, and if your ego is more secure than mine, you might allow yourself a more restrictive threshold.

## The adaptation

In 2025, I averaged 170 bpm for a 9:46 pace. I spent 88% of the race above 165 bpm. Peak was 178. I was near my ceiling from the first mile and fighting to stay there through the last.

In 2026: 162 bpm average, 9:22 pace, 32% of the race above 165. Peak of 169, a number Garmin's model thinks I haven't even reached threshold yet.

The run economy numbers are the most compelling. At matched cardiac output, I'm running 80 to 120 seconds per mile faster than I was a year ago. More output per beat. Fat oxidation doing the work anaerobic metabolism used to do.

The 2026 race shows it in real time. Heart rate climbed gradually, stabilized through miles one and two, and I still had a kick at the end. That's what a controlled effort looks like: not suffering less, but suffering with intention. Sub-30 was the goal, and the results proved [the training model](/the-thresholds-of-endurance/).

## The takeaway

If your watch gives you an LTHR that's higher than anything you've recorded in a genuinely hard effort, be skeptical. Run a race or a hard time trial. Take your average HR from the final 20 minutes. Compare it to what the algorithm says.

Then run easy at what feels easy, not where an algorithm tells you. Notice where your breathing shifts. Trust that signal.

The watch is a tool. The data it generates is yours to interrogate.
