Your body and mind are built to heal, regulate, and thrive through motion. The most important question isn't which movement you choose, but whether you move at all.

For me that movement is running and strength training. For others it might be walking, swimming, yoga, or pickleball (which has become ever so popular in recent years). The specific activity matters less than the habit itself. The best movement is the movement you'll keep doing.

When movement disappears from daily life, many systems gradually degrade. When it returns — in appropriate forms and doses — they recover. Strength returns. Balance returns. Adaptability returns. Movement isn't a lifestyle add-on. It's foundational.

**Biological Regulation**

When muscles contract, they send signals. Inflammation drops. Metabolism shifts. Your immune system responds. Your brain feels it. Joints circulate fluid. Bones respond to load. The heart and lungs adapt to the demands placed on them. Whether those signals come from a run, a walk, a bike ride, or a strength session, what matters is that the signal is sent.

Consistency beats heroics. Regular movement tells your tissues to stay strong. Long gaps followed by sudden spikes lead to injury because the body adapts precisely to what it is asked to do. Nothing more, nothing less. You will learn this the hard way if you ignore it.

**Nervous System Balance**

Gentle, rhythmic motion calms the stress response. You know the feeling: a walk that clears mental fog, a bike ride that dissolves tension after a difficult day. More intense or skill-based movement sharpens attention and builds confidence. The nervous system benefits from both ends of the spectrum.

Rest days matter, but too much rest works against you. Prolonged inactivity tends to worsen mood, increase stiffness, and make it harder to get moving again. A short walk or easy session on a difficult day is often enough to break that pattern.

**Meaning and Agency**

Movement reinforces agency. When you choose to move, especially when motivation is low, you make a quiet statement: I can act. I can participate in my own care. Each session is a small act of participation in your own wellbeing.

The right movement is the one that stays in your life when work is busy, motivation fades, or things get complicated. I've learned this repeatedly through injury, when other forms of movement become the bridge that keeps you healthy and eventually brings you back. Those gaps teach you what actually matters.

**The Long View**

Movement is something you practice across a lifetime, not a single program. The forms that work in one decade change in another. Running gives way to hiking. Cycling pairs with strength training. Long workouts shrink into shorter daily routines. The body does not require a perfect program. It requires regular reminders that life demands motion.
