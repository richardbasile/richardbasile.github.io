[The Thresholds of Endurance](/the-thresholds-of-endurance/) established the premise: two physiological boundaries, three training zones, and the case for keeping easy effort genuinely easy. Following is the first of three posts set to unpack what this premise looks like in practice, starting with the easy run.

The easy run is undeniably the foundation for running fitness, and many of us miss laying a solid foundation. A true easy run is ego crushing. It doesn't feel like training. It feels wrong. Pay attention to that feeling. It's the signal that affirms you're in the right zone.

## What easy actually means

Easy running lives below your aerobic threshold (LT1), the point where comfortable effort tips into meaningful effort. Below this threshold, your body builds the aerobic engine. Mitochondria, the cellular engines that convert oxygen into energy, multiply and become more efficient. Your muscles get better at burning fat as fuel, sparing glycogen for when you actually need it. Capillary density increases, improving oxygen delivery to working muscle. None of this happens quickly, and none of it is driven by effort. It's driven by volume and consistency at the right intensity. Going harder doesn't accelerate development; it taxes the engine without building it.

## Where many runners fall short

The problem is partly psychological. Running slowly in public feels like an admission of something. Watching your pace on a GPS watch and seeing a number that doesn't feel impressive is uncomfortable. So runners push, not into hard effort, just into something that feels more like work. A pace that requires a little focus. An effort that feels productive.

That's the trap. The zone between your aerobic and lactate thresholds accumulates fatigue without driving adaptation. It feels like training, but it quietly hollows out your ability to improve. If you feel stuck, putting in the miles but not seeing the gains, you're living in the black hole of comfortable discomfort. For me, this was the zone that kept me perpetually injured.

## Finding your aerobic threshold

LT1 is tricky to pin down precisely, and percentage-based heart rate formulas often miss it, placing your easy training above the threshold without it feeling like it. The 30-minute time trial outlined in Thresholds gives you a baseline: 80 to 90 percent of your lactate threshold heart rate, with 85 percent as a reasonable starting point.

What the number doesn't tell you is how to use it on any given day. Heart rate is sensitive to heat, fatigue, caffeine, stress, and poor sleep, all of which can push it higher at any given pace without a corresponding increase in actual effort. Chasing a heart rate ceiling on those days means slowing to a crawl or cutting the run short. Neither is necessary if you understand what the number is actually telling you.

The talk test is more reliable in practice. If you can hold a full, comfortable conversation, you're below LT1. If you're speaking in short phrases, you've crossed it. RPE 2 to 3. You should finish feeling like you could have gone further.

There's also a longer arc worth understanding. As your aerobic fitness develops, your easy pace will naturally increase at the same heart rate and effort level. The engine becomes more efficient, and the same perceived exertion produces more speed. This is what aerobic base building actually looks like from the inside: not a dramatic breakthrough, but a gradual, almost imperceptible shift in what easy feels like. If you've been training consistently and your easy pace has crept faster without your effort changing, that's the adaptation working.

Use the field test to establish a baseline. Use feel to govern every run. Watch the easy pace over months, not days. That's where the progress shows up.

## What to do when easy feels impossible

Heat, fatigue, stress, poor sleep, all of it pushes your heart rate up at any given pace. On those days, easy effort might mean slowing down more than feels reasonable, or taking walk breaks. Both are correct responses. Run/walk intervals aren't a consolation prize. They're a tool for keeping your effort where it belongs when your body is working harder than usual just to maintain.

If your heart rate won't come down no matter how slow you go, that's information too. It might be a recovery day instead of an easy run.

## The practical picture

Most of your running should be easy. If you're running four or five days a week, three or four of those days should live below LT1, typically 30 to 60 minutes, long enough to accumulate aerobic stimulus without generating meaningful fatigue. The pace will vary day to day depending on how you feel. The effort level shouldn't.

The goal isn't to avoid hard work. It's to concentrate it where it pays off, in your quality sessions, which only work if easy days have left you recovered enough to actually go hard. Easy running isn't the opposite of serious training. It's the foundation that makes serious training possible.

Run slower than you think you need to. Finish feeling like you left something in the tank. Do that consistently, and you build a more efficient engine.
