In [Raising the Ceiling](/raising-the-ceiling/), I talked myself into what looked like a contradiction. In the polarized model, hard effort is defined as everything above lactate threshold (LT2). If your LT2 sits at a known number, the implication seems to follow: any effort that doesn't push your heart rate above that number isn't hard enough to produce the target adaptation. If 5K pace intervals never get you there, maybe they don't belong in the program.

It's an understandable but incorrect reading of the model.

<!--more-->

## Your heart rate is late to the party

Heart rate is a cardiovascular response, not a real-time metabolic readout. When you accelerate into a hard interval, your muscles begin burning through glycogen and accumulating lactate immediately. Your heart rate may take 30 seconds to a minute or more to reflect actual demand, depending on intensity and individual fitness. During a 400-meter repeat at 5K pace, an effort that lasts perhaps 90 seconds, your cardiovascular system is still responding when the rep is already over.

This means heart rate is a lagging indicator, not a leading one. It tells you where your body has been, not where it is. For efforts shorter than two or three minutes, it is too slow a signal to be a useful real-time target. By the time your watch catches up, you've already been above your lactate threshold metabolically for the better part of a minute.

The adaptation is already happening. You just can't see it yet.

## Pace and feel are the primary signals

For short intervals, the targets that matter are pace and RPE. If I'm running 400-meter repeats at my 5K goal pace and the effort feels genuinely hard, labored breathing, no possibility of conversation, I'm already above threshold. The physiology is there. The number on the watch is lagging behind.

What you'd expect to see in a longer effort at the same pace is exactly what the polarized model predicts: heart rate climbing through and past your LT2 as the cardiovascular system catches up to the metabolic demand. An 800-meter repeat or a mile repeat at threshold pace gives enough time for that to happen. By the final minute of a hard mile, a runner working at the right intensity should be well above their LTHR. The lag closes as the effort extends.

This is why longer intervals are a useful confirmation. If your heart rate never climbs past your LT2 in a 10-minute tempo effort, you probably aren't working hard enough. If it settles meaningfully above it by the back half of a hard mile, that's the signal you were above threshold all along. The interval just needed time to show it.

## What fitness does to the equation

There's a second dimension worth tracking. Whether a given pace pushes you above LT2 depends on your current fitness, not the pace itself. Early in a training cycle, 5K pace might reliably drive heart rate past your LT2 given enough time in the effort. As your aerobic capacity improves, that same pace starts to feel more manageable. Heart rate at that effort drops. The session that was once hard drifts toward the black hole.

When fitness improves and easy days are genuinely easy, that drift is adaptation revealed. This is the signal to adjust your targets: increasing pace, extending intervals, or both. Heart rate is a confirmation of where your fitness is trending.

## How to use heart rate for intervals

During short reps, target pace and RPE. After the session, look at where heart rate landed in the final minutes of your longest efforts. If you're consistently peaking well below your LT2 on efforts that feel genuinely hard, something is off. Either the pace is too conservative or easy days have been running too high and left you carrying fatigue. If it's settling above your threshold by the back half of hard reps, the system is working.
